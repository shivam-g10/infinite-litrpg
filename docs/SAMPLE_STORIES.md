Ash Road began where Cinder Village ended.

Rowan kept walking until the blackened roofs and broken palisade slipped behind him, swallowed by the low rise of the road. He did not look back. The movement had been simple: leave the village, follow the gray trade road, continue away from the place the ash-raiders had struck. Yet each step seemed to draw a line through him, dividing what had been from what remained.

Ash lay across the road in thin sheets. It stirred beneath his boots and lifted in pale threads around his ankles before settling again. The road cut through old battlefields, though nothing in the gray ground announced where one ended or another began. Rowan saw only ruts, scattered stones, and stretches of earth darkened beneath the ash.

His rusted sword hung at his side, its weight familiar enough to be useful and light enough to forget. He kept one hand near the hilt without touching it. The copper coins in his possession made a small, dull weight elsewhere, but he gave them no thought. There was no reason to count them here.

Behind him, Cinder Village stayed behind.

Ahead, Ash Road stretched toward the other roads named on maps and spoken of by travelers: toward the capital road, toward the Black March. Rowan did not follow those names with his imagination. He had one destination. He would reach it by continuing.

The wind crossed the open ground and pressed ash against his face. Rowan narrowed his eyes. His body held together. Forty-two points of health remained, no more and no less. Eighteen points of mana waited beneath his skin, quiet and full. He did not spend either. The numbers were not comfort. They were measurements, and measurements could be trusted more than comfort.

He slowed where the road dipped.

Something had passed along the ash ahead. Rowan could see that much without calling on any skill. The surface held uneven marks, softened by wind but not erased. He studied them from a distance, refusing to assign them more meaning than they possessed. His plan was to read the ash trail. Reading was not discovery. The road showed disturbance; the road did not explain it.

He moved on.

The village grew smaller behind him, its burned edges flattening into the landscape. Ash-raiders had struck Cinder Village and withdrawn toward Ash Road. That fact remained sharp. It had happened. Rowan had seen the aftermath. Nyra Vale had been there as well, an uneasy ally whose presence did not become certainty merely because they had stood in the same ruined place.

He wanted to know what Nyra had noticed.

The desire tightened briefly between his shoulders. He let it remain only that: a goal, not an action. Nyra was not on the road with him. No voice carried across the gray distance. No answer came. Rowan continued alone.

No one could know who he had been.

A dark fleck crossed the distance ahead and vanished against the gray. Rowan watched the place where it disappeared, then lowered his gaze. The ash trail continued. So did he.
